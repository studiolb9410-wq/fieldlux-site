Judgment Engine

Auto-verdict on every install.

Drop in your projectors, pick the lens, point them at the target. It comes back with one of three calls:

READY · ships within mount/drift tolerance
AT_RISK · works on a best-case day, no margin
NOT_RECOMMENDED · expected to fail on a measurement that matters

Every call is backed by the checks that actually matter on site — pixel density, bright-enough coverage, target coverage, contrast margin, spill, blend hotspots. If one trips, you see exactly which metric tripped and what to do about it: move the rig, change the lens, bump the lumens. Thresholds and the reasoning live in the methodology section.

Prescription Engine

Concrete actions — not just warnings.

When brightness fails, we don't say "low lux". We say:

Move all 3 projectors 40 cm closer as a group
    — or —
upgrade to 15,000 lm units
    — or —
swap ELPLM15 for short-throw ELPLU04.

The "move X cm" and "bump to Y lumens" numbers come from the actual math — inverse-square, lens throw, blend-footprint geometry. Lens and gear swap suggestions use documented heuristics, all spelled out in the prescription card so you can audit the call.

When two fixes pull against each other — "come in for brightness" vs "pull back for coverage" — it picks the one that unblocks the binding metric and tells you why, not just what.

Multi-target Analysis

Analyze what you actually project onto.

Flat surfaces, room walls, imported building meshes, site photos — the same judgment engine runs on all four. Bring your model in as glTF, FBX, OBJ, or Collada. Lens-warped projection lands on the real mesh; when a ray misses, it falls back to a bounding-box centroid (that fallback's error budget is in the methodology section too).

Flat surface
Screen or scrim
Rectangular projection surface at a fixed throw. The baseline case — same judgment engine runs on all the rest.

Interior space

glTF · FBX · OBJ · Collada
